Reading 2 - Reflection

In reading Nesrine Malik's article, I found myself returning to a question that has always shaped the way I observe the world: how do we come to trust what we see? I have always been drawn to the idea that images carry meaning beyond what is immediately visible, that they shape emotion and perception in ways that often go unnoticed. However, this reading made me realize that this relationship between image and meaning is becoming increasingly unstable.

Malik suggests that we are now living within two overlapping visual realities: one grounded in real events, and another constructed through artificial, endlessly generated imagery. What unsettled me most is not simply the existence of these artificial images, but how seamlessly they blend into our everyday visual environment. The distinction between what is real and what is constructed is no longer clear, and this ambiguity begins to reshape how we experience the world itself.

Before engaging with this text, I often approached AI-generated visuals as something distant from “real” visual culture—something experimental, exaggerated, or even aesthetically interesting. But Malik shifts this perspective by emphasizing repetition and exposure. When artificial images become constant, they begin to alter not only what we see, but how we interpret everything we see. Reality does not disappear; instead, it becomes filtered through layers of artificial noise.

What I found particularly striking is the role of trust. Malik describes how images circulating through platforms like WhatsApp gain credibility not because they are accurate, but because they are shared by someone familiar. This made me think about how perception is not shaped by images alone, but by relationships, context, and pre-existing beliefs. In this sense, visual media is not just about representation—it is about reinforcement. We do not only see images; we accept them through frameworks that feel emotionally or socially secure.

Another idea that stayed with me is that AI is not neutral. It does not create in a vacuum, but reflects patterns already embedded within existing data. This means that it has the capacity to reproduce not only aesthetics, but also ideologies. What becomes concerning is how these ideologies are not presented directly, but are instead softened, beautified, and normalized through visual form. Certain political or social ideas can become more appealing simply because they are presented in a visually pleasing or nostalgic way.

At the same time, Malik connects this to the logic of the attention economy, where value is measured not by truth, but by engagement. This creates a space where images are designed to capture attention rather than communicate meaning. As a result, everything—real events, artificial content, emotional imagery, political narratives—exists within the same continuous flow. This constant mixture creates a kind of visual saturation that makes it difficult to distinguish urgency from distraction.

What resonates with me most is Malik's idea that the danger lies not in a lack of information, but in an overwhelming excess of it. When everything is visible, but nothing is stable, perception itself becomes uncertain. We are not disconnected from reality, but we are no longer anchored in it. The phrase “sleepwalking into disaster” feels less like ignorance and more like disorientation—a state where awareness exists, but clarity does not.

This reading has shifted the way I think about visual media. It is no longer only a tool for expression or communication, but also a force that can reshape perception at a structural level. It makes me question not only the authenticity of images, but also my own response to them. If visual experience can be so easily influenced, then critical awareness becomes essential—not just in understanding images, but in understanding how we are being guided to see them.

What this ultimately leaves me questioning is something more personal: if I begin to doubt what I see, then what do I rely on instead? If visual evidence itself becomes unstable, then perception is no longer something I can trust passively. It becomes something I have to actively question. In that sense, the issue is not only about AI or media, but about the fragility of our own sense of reality—and how easily it can be reshaped without us fully realizing it.
